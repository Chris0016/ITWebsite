How to Protect Yourself and Parts When Working With Electronics?
Christopher PerezMay , 2021 at am
share

 More often than not, components with electronics go bad. Fortunately, with some know-how servicing or replacing these components can be very easy (or hard). There are many hazards like electricity and fire that are present when working on systems even when they are off. Therefore, steps need to be taken and established to prevent and properly respond to any emergencies that can occur. Moreover, making sure that components are protected is also important when working on systems.

Tools

 Whether it's hardware or software tools, having the appropriate set of basic tools is fundamental. There are always specialized tools that could be necessary for specific systems, but you should have a set of general readily available hardware and software tools.

Tools: Hardware

 PC repair toolkits can be easily purchased online or at a physical store. These kits can vary in size but even the minimal kits contain the most useful hardware. One of the most basic of hardware tools is the screwdriver. There are many different head shapes so instead of buying one of each, it is most convenient to buy swappable heads. Something important to note is that many of these heads are magnetic so they shouldn't be used on magnetic drives since it can corrupt the memory.

Tweezers

Tweezers are useful when handling very small components or delicate components.

Multimeters

Multimeters are a commonly used tool for reading voltage. Multimeters are seen in scenarios of troubleshooting, where you may need to figure out if a power source is working effectively or the other way around.

Compressed Air

You can use compressed air to clean off dust and debris from systems.

Tools: Sofware

Although it is hardware, flash drives are a useful medium for storing things like operating systems that can be used to retrieve data in the event of failure of an operating system.

Protecting Yourself

 The most prominent danger when working on systems is Electrostatic Discharge (ESD). ESD is the rapid transfer of electrons from one mass to another when there exists a high potential difference between them. ESD can be seen when you feel a shock after touching someone. Before the shock you (in this case) may have done something like rub your feet on the carpet and built-up electrostatic charge. In an effort to achieve equilibrium the electrons flow from you to the other person you touch. Thus, the term discharge, a flow of electrons(electricity) from a body of high concentration to one that's lower.

-For more information on electricity click here.

The casual Shock you feel when you touch someone is about 3000v and only lasts for a fraction of a section. Unfortunately, that is more than enough to damage motherboard components like a chip that die at 200v.

How to prevent ESD

The solution to preventing ESD is minimizing the difference in electrostatic charge between you and the device your are working on. There are many ways to achieve that.

ESD Straps

ESD wrist straps or antistatic wrist bands work as a wristband with a cable that clips to the workstation you're working with. These wristbands have a capacitor embedded whose purpose is to dissipate charge.

ESD Mats

 ESD wrist wraps provide a free path for discharge to flow to ground (a capacitor). The same concept applies to ESD mats. In the case of ESD mats the grounding is done by connecting the attached cable to the ground plug on the power outlet. To use the mat, place the mat under the workstation.

Self Grounding

The term self-grounding encompasses different "DIY" methods for grounding. You can ground yourself by using a grounding strap and connecting it to the grounding plug on you power outlet.

Antistatic Bags

In the case where devices may be stored for a considerable amount of time or carried from place to place, antistatic bags can be used to shield equipment from building up static charge.

Humidifiers

Arid environments in combination with certain materials tend to favor the buildup of static charge. So having humidity levels set at about 45%-55% prevents the buildup of electric charge that is aided by dry environments.

Best Practices

Disconnect power before working with electrical systems.

Remove jewelry.

Proper Lifting Techniques. Use your knees not your back.

Electrical Fire safety. You should ensure that there is an electrical fire extinguisher nearby (and know where it is). You should periodically test that the extinguisher still functions.

Wear safety goggles and air filter mask in workplaces with a lot of dust and debris. Remember that using compressed air can help to remove dust and debris.

Organize cables. Do not leave cables out in the open and disorganized where they can be stepped on by someone. Ensure that cables are bundled together and use cable mats or rubber mats to cover them.

Calling for Help

 There are certain devices that hold charge even after powering them off, such as batteries. It is often a good choice not to work with high voltage equipment unless you are certified to work with them. These components will usually have a sign indicating high voltage. Instead, the best practice is to call for someone that is knowledgeable about working with these parts as these can be very dangerous.
